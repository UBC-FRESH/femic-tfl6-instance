1.0 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Purpose 
The Tree Farm Licence (TFL) agreement requires the Licensee to submit for Ministry of 
Forests (MoF) approval a Management Plan.  Revised every five years, the Management Plan 
provides objectives, goals and strategies to guide management activities until the next 
revision.  The Management Plan and associated Timber Supply Analysis are used by the MoF 
Chief Forester to determine the Allowable Annual Cut (AAC).  The Management Plan provides 
direction for the preparation of operational plans.  The Plan is focused on results and 
outcomes so that the Licensee, Western Forest Products Limited (WFP) can evaluate 
performance and continuously improve management practices.  The Plan contains 
measurable parameters in the form of indicators that will monitor the achievement of 
sustainable forest management objectives for the period November 1, 2001 to October 31, 
2006.  It is also intended to form the foundation for Forest Stewardship Council and Canadian 
Standards Association sustainable forestry certifications. It is expected that further 
amendments may be proposed for this plan to fully address FSC and CSA requirements for 
forest certification 
1.2 Description of the TFL 
The Quatsino Tree Farm Licence 6 (TFL 6) is located on northern Vancouver Island in the 
vicinity of Quatsino Sound (Figure 1). The total area of the TFL is approximately 198,113 
hectares of which 156,769 hectares is Crown land and 41,334 hectares are timber licences or 
fee simple lands owned by WFP.  A detailed summary of the TFL land status and mature 
timber volumes is in Table 1.  The timber harvesting landbase is 149,747 ha.  Figure 2 shows 
the ownership associated with TFL 6.  The fee simple (private) lands and lands controlled by 
the licensee are encompassed in 
Managed Forests (MF) 29 and 
31.  Maps and area summaries 
for MF 29 and 31 are found in 
Appendix VI.  The TFL is made 
up of two geographically 
separated management areas.  
Block 1 is the largest 
management unit and includes 
watersheds that drain westward 
into Quatsino Sound or the 
Pacific Ocean between Quatsino 
Sound and San Josef Bay.  Block 
2 includes watersheds that drain 
northeastward into Queen 
Charlotte Strait between Fort 
Rupert and Port McNeill plus 
those that drain westward into the 
Waukwaas watershed and Rupert 
Arm.

The topography within the TFL is variable.  Relatively low relief, undulating terrain 
characterizes the easterly and westerly sections of the TFL.  The central and inland portions 
of the TFL are characterized as mountainous and steep.  Numerous rivers and streams drain 
the licence area.  Most streams support significant anadromous and resident fish populations.  
Large animals, notably, Columbia black-tailed deer, cougars and black bears are abundant 
throughout the licence area.  Numerous other large and small mammals, amphibians, fish and 
birds can also be found.  These are identified in Appendix XXII.

The forests of TFL 6 lie within the wetter maritime Coastal Western Hemlock biogeoclimatic 
zone.  Annual precipitation levels reach 3,000 to 5,000 mm.  The climate is characterized by 
mild, wet winters with daily mean minimum temperatures December to February of 0 to 2 
degrees C.  Summers are generally cool and moist with mean daily maximum temperatures of 
18 to 20 degrees C during July and August.  However, local climates within the TFL can be 
significantly different due to topographic influences and the movement of low cloud and fog 
from offshore onto northern Vancouver Island.  The dominant timber species is western 
hemlock, which occurs in conifer stands mixed with varying amounts of amabilis fir and 
western red cedar.  Lesser amounts of Sitka spruce, yellow cedar, Douglas fir, alder, shore 
pine and mountain hemlock also occur. 
There are seven communities within or adjacent to the licence area.  Their livelihood and 
stability depends mostly or in part on the economic activities generated on the North Island.  
These are the communities of Port McNeill, Port Alice, Port Hardy, Holberg, Coal Harbour, 
Winter Harbour, and Quatsino as noted in Figure 3.  WFP harvesting operations in TFL 6 and 
adjacent forest licences, and the associated dissolving pulp manufacturing facility in Port 
Alice, are the major employment source for north Vancouver Island.  There is also economic 
activity from other forest licensees, fish farming, commercial and recreational fishing and 
expanding tourism.

1.3 History and Progress 
TFL 6 is held and managed by Western Forest Products Limited (WFP).  Western Forest 
Products Limited is a wholly owned subsidiary of Doman Industries Limited.  
Block 1 is the original TFL 6 area granted to Western’s predecessor, British Columbia Pulp & 
Paper Company Limited as Quatsino Forest Management Licence 6 on October 26, 1950.  
Block 2 is the former Block 4 of Tree Farm Licence 25 which was granted to another 
predecessor company, Alaska Pine and Cellulose Limited, on May 21, 1958.  On October 1, 
1998 these two areas were consolidated into a new TFL 6 made up of Block 1 (former TFL 6) 
and Block 2 (former TFL 25 Block 4).  Now in its fifth decade of operation, WFP and 
predecessors have responsibly managed the licence for its long-term sustainability.  WFP is 
proud of the efforts of our employees and contractors in helping to sustain this important 
generator of employment and crown revenues.  Appendix I lists amendments to the licence 
area that have occurred since 1950.  
The following accomplishments indicate the licensee’s willingness and ability to manage more 
than 198,000 hectares of lands and forests in Tree Farm Licence 6 in a spirit of co-operation 
with government agencies, its employees and a multi-faceted public.  Although site specific 
management cycles will vary considerably, Figure 4 illustrates a generalized Forest 
Management Cycle.

1.4 Licence Holder and Administration  
Western Forest Products Limited is a wholly owned subsidiary of Doman Industries Limited 
(Doman).  Doman is a coastal B.C. forest products company involved in timber harvesting, 
saw milling, value-added lumber remanufacturing, dissolving sulphite pulp production and 
kraft pulp production.  Doman’s nine saw mills have an annual lumber production capacity of 
1.1 billion board feet.  Major markets include the U.S.A., Japan and Europe.  Doman also 
owns one sulphite pulp mill at Port Alice, which has an annual production capacity of 160,000 
air-dried metric tonnes (ADMT).  Doman has a kraft pulp mill at Woodfibre with an annual 
production capacity of 240,000 ADMT.  Major markets for pulp include North America, Europe 
and the Far East.    
Western Forest Products Limited is responsible for timber harvesting and reforestation on 
approximately 885,000 ha of Crown and private forest lands located in coastal British 
Columbia.  WFP has an annual timber harvest of approximately 4.2 million cubic metres under 
its tenures, which include three Tree Farm Licences, seven Forest Licences, 5 Managed 
Forests and 125 Timber Licences.  The Company’s AAC together with log trading and other 
log supply agreements provides for approximately 85% of the fibre requirements for the 
Company’s manufacturing plants.  The remainder of the wood is purchased on the open 
market. 
WFP’s forest operations are organized into three regions.  North Vancouver Island Region, 
with a regional office in Port McNeill, administers harvesting and reforestation activities in the 
north end of Vancouver Island.  Mainland/Islands Region, with a regional office in Campbell 
River, administers harvesting and reforestation activities in numerous north and central coast 
operations as well as operations on the Queen Charlotte Islands, southern Vancouver Island 
and the lower mainland coast.  Nootka Region, with a regional office in Gold River, 
administers harvesting and reforestation activities on the central, west coast of Vancouver 
Island.  WFP’s corporate head office is located in Vancouver.  Appendix XVIII contains a copy 
of the Corporate Annual Statutory Report for 2000.  The company’s overall utilization of forest

2.0 SUSTAINABLE FOREST MANAGEMENT PRINCIPLES 
2.1 Sustainable Forest Management Policy  
WFP’s forest management is guided by three overarching principles.  These are:  
• Our operations must be economically viable.  Our company must operate in a financially 
successful manner to meet shareholders expectations of a fair return on investment and 
to meet social and environmental commitments;   
• Our actions must be socially beneficial to local, regional and First Nation’s communities.  
WFP is committed to respecting, understanding and supporting First Nations, community 
and employee aspirations for stability and certainty;  
• Our activities must be environmentally appropriate.  WFP is committed to the protection 
of the environment and the sustainable development of the resources under our 
stewardship through sound forestry and environmental management practices that meet 
or exceed government standards. 
• Under these principles, our company is committed to:  
 
• Establishing harvest levels that balance the carrying capacity of forest ecosystems 
with social, environmental, and economic considerations. 
 
• Utilizing forest practices appropriate to maintaining, social, environmental, and 
economic objectives 
 
• Engaging a team of dedicated professional foresters and planners committed to 
implementing and practicing sustainable forest management 
 
• Continually enhancing our ability to plan and manage by promoting applied research 
and using leading edge inventory and modeling systems. 
 
• Planning for the long-term in an integrated manner to incorporate the full range of 
forest values including soil, water, fish and wildlife, archaeological, scenic resources 
and biological diversity. 
 
• Maintaining a practical, registered management system to guide and direct 
environmental aspects of company operations 
 
• Performing regular, internal and external audits to ensure compliance with forest 
practices legislation and the commitments made in this SFM statement. 
 
• Committing to a level of compliance that meets or exceeds legal requirements and 
satisfies public expectations 
 
• Continuously improving all aspects of our forest practices though adaptive 
management. 
 
• Communicating our performance to stakeholders and the public on a regular basis 
 
• Maintaining biological diversity and conservation objectives at regional, landscape 
and stand levels. 
 
• Sustaining, enhancing and protecting forest ecosystem functions. 
 
• Maintaining soil productivity and water quality by harvesting with environmentally 
appropriate systems. 
 
• Researching habitat needs of fish and wildlife species and populations and

2.3 WFP’s Forest Certification Initiatives 
The stewardship principles are essential components in the Company’s objective to be 
certified under the Forest Stewardship Council Principles and Criteria (FSC), Canada’s 
National Sustainable Forest Management System Standard, CAN/CSA-Z809-96 (CSA) and 
ISO 14001 Environmental Management System Standard (ISO).  Linkages between WFP’s

FSC, CSA and ISO are embodied in this Management Plan and articulated in the Plan 
objectives, strategies and performance targets.   
Measurable performance targets are set and monitored as a means of meeting the Plan 
objectives. Some targets will be established and reviewed annually while others will be set 
and reviewed periodically. TFL 6 operations were certified to ISO 14001 standards in April 
2000 by QMI, an ISO certifier.  Appendix XVI provides details of the ISO 14001 program.  TFL

Forestry, an accredited certifier, against the Forest Stewardship Council’s Principles and 
Criteria earlier in 2000.  A certification decision has not been reached at this time, and work 
continues in addressing correction actionrequired that are outstanding from the 2000 
assessment work.  As part of the CSA process in January 2001 a Community Advisory Group 
was formed to help define local values, goals, indicators and objectives for TFL 6 and 
adjacent company tenures.  As the work of the group is still in its infancy and values, goals, 
indicators and objectives have not been developed yet.  It is expected that the information 
attained from this local advisory group will be incorporated into the Management Plan as an 
amendment along with changes required as a result of further FSC related requirements. 
Depending on the level of FSC and CSA changes required, the company may consider a 
separate plan as these initiatives cover both TFL 6 and other adjacent tenures. 
 WFP will report on performance in achieving targets and Plan objectives.  The TFL annual 
report will be one of the main reporting mechanisms used.  Others include corporate reports to 
shareholders and audits (both external and internal).

3.1.1 Forest Cover 
The most recent field inventory was done between 1968 and 1970. Subsequently, this 
inventory has been updated to account for the area harvested and regenerated as well as 
other changes. This updated inventory was used for MP 8. Because of its age, it has no 
formal letter of approval. However at the time it was done, methodology followed MoF policies 
and procedures. 
WFP has recently begun a new Vegetation Resource Inventory (VRI) project for TFL 6 that is 
planned for completion in 2001. Funding for the VRI will come from WFP’s 5-year agreement 
with Forest Renewal BC.  Preliminary discussions with the MoF Region have been undertaken 
(May 11,1999).  Photo interpretation and quick plot ground samples were completed in 1999.  
A more intensive ground survey program will be completed in 2000. 
3.1.2 Environmentally Sensitive Areas 
Areas assessed as sensitive or valuable for other resource values have been delineated 
through inventories conducted before and since MP 8.  Management objectives reflecting the 
presence of these areas are in other sections of this document, including terrain stability and 
soil sensitivity, wildlife habitat, riparian and recreational reserves. 
3.1.3 Operability 
Recently 1:20,000 scale operability mapping has been updated for the TFL.  The terms of 
reference for the new operability mapping were submitted to the Ministry of Forests in 
February 1999 and approved by the Regional Manager in June 1999. A field review with 
district staff was undertaken in October 1999 and final adjustments were made to the mapping 
in November 1999.  Approximately 98% of operable forest types can be harvested with 
conventional cable and ground based methods.  Helicopter or skyline systems will be utilized 
to harvest the remaining 2% of the operable landbase.  Appendix XV illustrates the first 20 
years of block sequencing used by the timber supply model to generate the “current 
management” harvest flow in the timber supply analysis (Appendix III). 
3.1.4 Recreation 
The current recreation features inventory was completed in 1993.  The inventory consists 
1:50,000 map sheets showing recreation feature classes, and a report describing the 
recreation and biophysical features and a management classification.  This inventory has 
classified and mapped the licence area into five Recreation Opportunity Spectrum Classes 
based on access, feature significance and recreational use. 
In 1995, WFP submitted a subzoning proposal to the Land Use Co-ordination Office for 
Special Management Zone 2.  Part of the proposal outlined a West Coast recreation and 
biodiversity reserve from Grant Bay to Cape Scott Park.  In 1996 and 1997 the outer West 
Coast was evaluated for a potential trail route between Grant Bay and Cape Scott.  The 
evaluation reaffirmed earlier work by the British Schools Exploration Society, to locate a trail 
route connecting recreational and scenic values in the area but that there would be issues 
with the trail route depending on the shape that recreational development would take.  In 
1997/98, the route was further evaluated by an engineering consultant and a number of 
challenges (stream and river crossings, treacherous terrain, inaccessible beaches on high and 
storm tides, etc.) were identified.  Due to the significant construction cost estimates, the 
program was not deemed feasible.  Also, the Cape Scott and proposed North Coast trails 
provide ample amounts of remote hiking and other more accessible routes fill an important

demand niche.  WFP has therefore changed its philosophy on the recreation vision for the 
area.  Future development will focus on providing high quality access routes to less hazardous

As interest in wildlife broadened, so did inventory efforts.  D. Blood & Associates completed a 
TFL 6 biodiversity overview in 1996 that has been used for management purposes.  The 
overview provides a comprehensive inventory of flora and fauna that are likely found within

the long-term biodiversity of the area.   
1. Meeting landscape goals for old seral representativeness and protecting under 
represented plant communities, 
2. Maintained or recruiting habitat for species of conservation interest (red and blue

3. Protecting aquatic habitat for vulnerable fish stocks, and

3.1.12 High Conservation Value Forests 
High Conservation Value Forests are defined as those forests that possess one or more of 
the following attributes: 
/g216/g32Forest areas containing globally, regionally or nationally significant: 
• Concentrations of biodiversity values (e.g. endemism, endangered species refugia); 
and/or 
• Large landscape level forests, contained within, or containing the management unit, 
where viable populations of most if not all naturally occurring species exist in natural 
patterns of distribution and abundance 
/g216/g32Forest areas that are in or contain, rare, threatened or endangered ecosystems 
/g216/g32Forest areas that provide basic services of nature in critical situations (e.g. watershed 
protection, erosion control) 
/g216/g32Forest areas fundamental to meeting basic needs of communities (e.g. subsistence, 
health) and/or critical to local communities’ traditional cultural identity (areas of cultural,

ecological, economic or religious significance identified in cooperation with such local 
communities). 
While the term High Conservation Value Forests (HCVF) is new, the concept behind it is not.  
WFP has been engaged in various forms of public consultation over the past three decades 
which have identified several attributes that are considered HCVF within the Licence area.  
Many of the HCVF attributes identified thus far are included within the inventories discussed 
earlier.  Other attributes that have not been specifically identified in current inventories (rare, 
threatened, or endangered ecosystems or plants) can generally be flagged by correlating the 
comprehensive ecosystem inventory completed by Lewis with the information provided by the 
B. C. Conservation Data Centre. 
During the term of the plan, WFP will identify and locate known HCVF attributes and develop 
management strategies to provide for these attributes. The management strategies will be 
incorporated into Landscape Unit and operational planning processes.  It is likely  the 
management strategies will go beyond the current planning policies and processes guided by 
the company’s scientific advisors.  It is expected that the process of developing local 
definitions, mapping and management strategies will be ongoing and subject to continual 
evolution as new information and new techniques are learned.

3.2.5 Adaptive Management 
Forestry plans must recognize limited knowledge and uncertainty.  For this reason, ecosystem 
management plans are based upon adaptive management principles.  These include the 
maintenance of ecosystems and habitats across a landscape, consideration of land 
management impacts at both the stand and landscape levels and striking a balance between 
timber and other resources.  As further knowledge is gained, management practices will adapt 
and utilize the knowledge in the development of future management strategies.

4.2 Environmentally Appropriate Forest Management Principle 
4.2.1 Conservation of Biological Diversity Criteria 
Biological diversity (or biodiversity) is the diversity of plants, animals and other living 
organisms in all their forms and levels of organization, and includes the diversity of genes, 
species and ecosystems, as well as the evolutionary and functional processes that link them 
(FPC, Biodiversity Guide book, September 1995).  An ecosystem management approach that 
maintains suitable habitat conditions for native species will be used as a surrogate to 
maintaining biodiversity.  Both coarse and fine filter approaches will be utilized to achieve 
biodiversity objectives. 
Coarse filter strategies include the maintenance of riparian habitats, areas of old growth and a 
variety of patch sizes across a landscape.  At the stand level coarse filter strategies include 
maintaining wildlife tree patches, snags, downed woody debris and deciduous trees. 

--- page 46 ---
 
TFL 6 - Sustainable Forest Management Plan  Page 38 
Fine filter strategies deal with the identification and maintenance of habitats for known 
sensitive plant and animal species.  Special consideration will be given to habitat 
requirements for red and blue listed species. Measures, which may be used to achieve fine 
filter strategies, include: 
1) the creation of Wildlife Habitat Areas 
2) establishment of Old Growth Management Areas in critical habitat zones 
3) use of interim measures, as prescribed in the Identified Wildlife Management 
Strategy to protect red and blue listed species and their habitats. 
4) Further work to be completed as part of the biological rationale for the company’s 
FSC certification program.

Within the TFL, areas in Varney, Spruce Bay, and Misty Lake have been identified as 
having unique ecological values.  The 120 hectare Varney Gene Pool Reserve was 
established by the company to protect one of the most northerly sites of Douglas fir 
on Vancouver Island. The Spruce Bay genetic reserve encapsulates large old growth 
Sitka spruce within the Spruce Bay Old Growth Reserve.  The unique giant black 
stickle- back (fish) of Misty Lake are protected within the Misty Lake Ecological 
Reserve and Provincial Park.  The park is being removed from the TFL and the other 
areas are excluded from the harvesting land base. 
A second component of ecosystem diversity is seral stage.  Currently more than 40% 
of the timber harvesting landbase is covered with mature or old seral stages.  Over 
time a trend toward a more balanced age class distribution will occur as a result of 
harvesting.  Spatial modeling tools will be utilized to ensure the harvest pattern 
maintains sufficient quantities of natural habitat at each seral stage across the 
landscape.  Areas of old growth, as defined by the FPC Biodiversity Guidebook, will 
be designated as Old Growth Management Areas (OGMAs) under Landscape Unit 
Planning.  This process began in May 2000 in cooperation with the Ministry of Forests 
and the Ministry of Environment, Lands and Parks.  It is anticipated that the process 
of identifying OGMAs and their specific objectives will be completed in 2002 for the 
Plan area.  
A third component of ecosystem diversity is patch size.  Wind and to a much lesser 
extent fire resulting from lightning strikes, are the main natural disturbance factors in 
the forests of TFL 6.  Disturbance events have historically created a range of patch 
sizes up 250 ha.  As per the FPC, Biodiversity Guidebook recommendation, 
development will attempt to achieve a distribution of patch sizes across the landscape 
of 30-40% in patches <40ha, 30-40% in patches 40-80 ha and 20-40% in patches 81-
250 ha. 
Protected areas adjacent to and within TFL 6 are mapped.  Other areas reserved 
from harvest, as per the FPC, include riparian reserve zones on fish streams, 
ungulate winter ranges and environmentally sensitive areas.  With respect to riparian 
areas, road construction will be carefully monitored to reduce sediment where stream 
crossings are required.  Timing windows will be observed for road construction 
around fish streams.  Where required appropriate riparian management areas or 
buffers will be established to maintain the integrity of riparian reserves. 
SFM indicators for area reserved, seral stage distribution, patch size distribution and 
environmental inventories and assessments have been established which will guide 
planning and forest management activities.  These indicators will be reported on in 
the management plan and other pertinent reports.

“A Strategy for Managing Wildlife” in TFL 6 (Appendix XXII) was developed by 
consultant Laurie Kremsater in late 1999.  The wildlife strategy for North Vancouver 
Island Region (NVIR) focuses on maintaining terrestrial forest dependent organisms 
across the tenure.  To do this the Company will employ Kremsater’s three main

Forest health is a key element in the maintenance of ecosystem condition and 
productivity.   WFP will maintain a forest health program that is designed to protect 
the licence area from damage by insects, disease, abiotic factors and fire.  A 
proactive strategy will be pursued in detection and control.  The goals of the program 
are to: 
• Minimize the loss of timber in operable merchantable stands; 
• Maintain the forest productivity and health of immature stands by monitoring 
insects and disease activity and implementing control action when needed; 
• Salvage insect, disease, wind or fire damaged merchantable timber quickly; 
• Minimize the time between felling, harvesting and processing of timber 
The forests of TFL 6 have been relatively free of major insect or disease infestations.  
There have been no major catastrophic outbreaks causing significant unsalvaged

WFP’s basic silviculture program will conform to the FPC, Silviculture Practices 
Regulation.  A description of the basic silviculture program is as follows: 
Site preparation is not required for initial reforestation in most areas with the 
exception of spot accumulations of yarding slash along roads.  However, in some 
areas containing very heavy concentrations of cedar slash, broadcast site preparation 
is required.  This is carried out using either prescribed burning or mechanical 
methods.  Roadside accumulations that prevent planting to desired stocking densities 
will be piled.  Piles may be burned or left as coarse woody debris and/or wildlife 
habitat.  Roadside accumulations account for less than 1% of the reforestation area. 
Planting is the main method of reforestation and is normally supplemented by natural 
regeneration.  Local seed is utilized for planting stock.  To achieve desired stocking 
density trees are planted in almost every cutblock.  The number of trees planted will 
depend on the amount of advanced regeneration retained after harvesting, expected 
natural seed in, brush hazard, ecosystem class and browse hazard.  Seedlings are 
obtained from a number of container nurseries located on Vancouver Island including 
WFP's nursery in Saanich.  Wherever available, “A” class improved seed is used.  
Much of the Hw, Cw and Fd seed used is “A” class and produced at WFP’s localized 
seed orchards in Saanich.  Yellow cedar stecklings are produced from rooted 
cuttings.  Wild seed collections or purchases are made to address any deficiencies in 
short-term seed supplies.  A ten year supply of seed is normally maintained in storage 
for most species. 
Mechanical site preparation and/or spot applications of herbicide may be required 
prior to fill-planting. Herbicide applications are planned as per Pest Management 
Plans or Pesticide Use Permits, which are discussed under brush, control.  
Historically less than 4% of the reforestation area requires fill-planting. 
Silviculture surveys are performed on all regenerating cutblocks to monitor progress 
towards achieving free growing status.  Information collected includes species; total 
number of stems and number that are well-spaced; brush species and distribution; 
and qualitative remarks.  The results are used to prescribe follow-up silviculture 
treatments if necessary.  Generally one survival survey and a minimum of one 
regeneration survey are undertaken prior to a final free to grow assessment.  Survey 
results are reported to the MoF via Major Licence Silviculture Information System 
(MLSIS) reports. 
An integrated pest management approach will be used when determining treatment 
thresholds, and treatments for brush control.  The majority of TFL 6 is covered under 
either a five year Pest Management Plan (Jeune Landing Operation) or Pesticide Use 
Permits (Holberg Operation) which are approved by the MOELP.  These documents 
specify when and how brush control treatments will be undertaken, consultation 
protocols, environmental protection measures and monitoring.  The Company is 
striving to reduce the amount of pesticides used.  Where feasible, alternatives to 
herbicides are used to control brush.  Table 15 indicates the Five-Year Basic

The total area of TFL 6 is approximately 198,000 ha.  Of this total, 185,500 ha are 
considered productive.  Existing roads, trails and landings occupy roughly 2,500 ha or 
1% of the productive land.  In 1999 a reclassification of the TFLs landbase was 
completed that reflects the physical accessibility and an economic availability of the 
land.  Lands classified as physically inaccessible would include lands where there is a 
physical barrier or terrain constraint leaving accessibility virtually impossible mostly 
due to environmental sensitivity.  Approximately 11,600 ha of productive area was 
identified and were removed from the timber harvesting landbase due to physical 
accessibility.  Approximately 950 hectares have been removed from the timber 
harvesting landbase as economically unavailable under current conditions. 
One of the primary goals of harvest planning is to minimize impacts on the net 
productive landbase.  Permanent and temporary access structures are planned to 
minimize the total amount of road required to safely and economically harvest the 
timber.  For the period 1995-99 the percent of permanent access within conventional 
cutblocks has averaged 4.6%. 
Mainline and branch road prisms during this period have been on average 11 metres 
wide.  Spur roads have averaged 9 meters.  Future roads are forecast to occupy 
2,800 ha.  The following practices are undertaken to minimize the loss of productive 
area. 
• Sensitive road cut slopes and fill slopes are/or planted with trees and grass 
seeded 
• Temporary access structures and abandoned roads are debuilt wherever 
possible 
• Excess material generated by road building on slopes greater than 60% is 
generally end hauled or keyed in to minimize side-casting 
• Historically over steepened fill slopes are rehabilitated through side cast 
pullback and planted with trees and grass seeded 
Terrain stability assessments are completed where harvesting is proposed in areas of

Carbon Sequestration refers to the provision of long-term storage of carbon in the 
terrestrial biosphere, underground, or the oceans so that the buildup of carbon dioxide 
(the principal greenhouse gas) concentration in the atmosphere is reduced or slowed. 
In some cases, this is accomplished by maintaining or enhancing natural processes. 
Global carbon cycles are not fully understood. However, it is believed that the global 
carbon cycle is out of balance. The increased atmospheric CO
2 level is making global 
climatic change more likely.  There has also been a general belief that the burning of 
fossil fuels contributes to increased atmospheric CO2 levels.  While the burning of 
fossil fuel is necessary to carrying out forest management activities the levels of 
consumption will be monitored relative to the production levels.   
Establishment and maintenance of forests is an important aspect of the terrestrial 
carbon sequestral. As forests grow they convert carbon dioxide in the air to carbon 
stored in trees. Forests form a large terrestrial sink for carbon. Harvesting will be 
followed by prompt reforestation. 
The indicators for carbon sequestration will be monitored on an annual basis and 
reported in the annual reports.

4.2.5 High Conservation Value Forests Criteria 
The term "High Conservation Value Forest" or HCVF originates from Principle 9 of the Forest 
Stewardship Council's ten Principles and Criteria.  Management of HCVF focuses on the 
precautionary maintenance or enhancement of attributes such as significant concentrations of 
biodiversity, ecosystems at risk, watersheds, unstable terrain, or fundamental economic or 
cultural needs of local communities or First Nations. 
Management of attributes identified as sensitive to forest management has been ongoing for 
many years.  Management strategies for them have been described in previous plans or other 
sections herein.  
TFL 6 is part of a defined forest area for the purposes of certification under the Forest 
Stewardship Council.  A summary paper entitled “Statement on High Conservation Value 
Forests of North Vancouver Island Region” was drafted in 1999 and submitted to the public, 
stakeholders, and First Nations for review and input.  From the feedback received a more 
comprehensive document entitled “High Conservation Forests North Vancouver Island 
Region” was developed and circulated for further input. The most recent version of the 
strategy can be found in Appendix XX.  Within this strategy, proposed HCVF attributes are 
identified and strategies specifically developed for the management and monitoring of them. 
The Licensee has enlisted the services of a team of specialists (biologists & ecologists) to 
review this strategy and refine the approach taken on High Conservation Value Forests.  It is 
expected that this review will result in an updating of the related appendices.  This review and 
updating is expected to be completed by the end of 2001.  This strategy is subject to continual 
review and updating as new information is learned, consultation continues, and society’s 
values change over time.

5.0 Sustainable Forest Management Plan Consultation 
The preparation of Management Plan 9 for TFL 6 has followed the old TFL Management Plan 
process.  The process laid out a 30 month time line leading up to the approval of a new plan 
and included the following: a public review of the current plan, submission of a review strategy 
for the new plan, a Statement of Management, Options, Objectives and Procedures 
(SMOOP)(Appendix XXIII), an Employment and Economic Opportunity report (EEO)(Appendix 
XXIV), an information package, a timber supply analysis and 20 year projection, and a draft 
MP.  This process was initiated for TFL 6 in July 1998 following direction from the MoF 
Vancouver Region calling for a public review of the current plan. 
WFP developed a public and stakeholder review strategy for MP 9 late in 1998 and carried out 
the public review of the current plan in August and September of 1998.  Advertisements were 
placed in local newspapers and on the Company website inviting comments on current 
performance under MP 8 and suggestions for consideration.  In addition, letters were sent to 
250 stakeholders advising them of the review and an invitation to comment. 
MP 8 was on review in Port McNeill (WFP and MoF), Port Alice, Holberg, Nanaimo (MoF), 
Victoria (MoF) and Vancouver (WFP, Head Office) from July 26, 1998 to September 30, 1998.  
Seven responses were received on the Statement of Management Objectives, Options and 
Procedures (SMOOP). Generally responses were very favourable and highlighted the 
company’s history and strong community ties.  One local resident was concerned with road 
deactivation limiting access into areas for honey collection.  The resident was satisfied that 
several opportunities would still exist for honey collection.  Other comments were related to 
optimizing the value of the wood produced, forest management activities above Port Alice and 
windthrow management.  There were no significant changes required to the SMOOP.  
A revised MP 9 stakeholder and public review strategy was submitted with the Draft MP as 
part of Appendix X.  Open houses were held in Port McNeill, Port Alice, Holberg and Port 
Hardy.  WFP staff also met with municipal councils in the above-mentioned communities and 
with the three First Nations whose traditional territory encompasses TFL 6.  A summary report 
was prepared and is appended to the proposed MP in Appendix X. A total of 163 persons 
attended the 13 sessions. A total of 14 written comments were received as well as the 
Regional Manager’s comments.

6.4 Impacts Due to Implementation of Management Plan 9 
6.4.1 Harvest and Employment Levels 
The Timber Supply Analysis (Appendix III) for MP 9 proposes an Allowable Annual Cut of 
1,459,000 m3.  This proposed harvest level is 2% less than the MP 8, which was 1,489,000 
m3.  This decrease is in line with the proposed step down that was outlined within MP 8.   
Due to the increased complexity of forest management activities and the small decrease in 
AAC, there are no significant changes anticipated to the employment levels as a result of 
implementation of this plan. 
6.4.2 Non-Timber Values 
 
Management Plan 9 is the first plan to fully incorporate the results of implementation of the 
Forest Practices Code.  MP 9 is also the first plan that has been designed to be in draft 
compliance with both FSC and CSA certification systems.  While non-timber values were 
mentioned in previous MPs, MP 9 has taken a more comprehensive approach. These values 
include wildlife, streams, recreation, visual quality, biological diversity, non-timber forest 
products, soil, cultural, social, and high conservation value forests.  MP 9 has also outlined in 
detail how each of these values will be monitored.   
6.4.3 General Impacts of Implementation 
WFP believes that the implementation of this plan will aid in giving the company greater 
economic stability.  With greater economic stability, more economic opportunities would be 
available for the communities that are directly tied to the operations within TFL 6.  
It is not anticipated that the implementation of this plan will have any further negative impacts 
over the current plan.
