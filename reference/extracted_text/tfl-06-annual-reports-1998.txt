Quatsino Tree Farm Licence 6 was granted to Western Forest Products’ 
predecessor, British Columbia Pulp and Paper Company Limited, as Quatsino 
Forest Management Licence on October 25 , 1950.  On October 1, 1998, the 
Minister of Forests approved a significant change to the original Tree Farm 
Licence.  Block 4 of TFL 25 was eliminated and amalgamated into TFL 6 as 
Block 2.  This Block comprises the watersheds of Queen Charlotte Strait 
between Fort Rupert and Port McNeill, and portions of the Quatsino Sound 
watershed.  Alaska Pine and Cellulose Limited, another predecessor 
company, was granted this Licence on May 22, 1958.  
 
This Annual Report records accomplishments and data for the amende d TFL 
6 for the entire year except for detailed scale data.  Scaled volumes reported 
reflect the amendment date of October 1, 1998.  If you have comments on 
this report, please contact the Chief Forester at (604) 665-6224.

1.0 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Statement of Stewardship 
Quatsino Tree Farm Licence 6 encompasses 201 500 ha of coastal forest on northern Vancouver 
Island.  It has been managed by Western Forest Products Limi ted and its predecessors for 48 
years.  More than 60 million cubic metres of wood have been produced from these lands in that 
time.  Forty percent of the total operable area of 157 000 ha remains in mature or old growth forest.  
Internal and external audit s and inspections indicated that a high standard of forest management 
was maintained during the year. Western Forest Products’ Forest Operations were fully compliant 
with the Forest Practices Code and in many instances exceeded requirements.  
Rural Vancouve r Island communities, including Winter Harbour, Quatsino, Holberg, Port Hardy, 
Coal Harbour, Port Alice, and Port McNeill continued to receive significant economic benefits from 
the management activities on TFL 6.  Despite extended shutdowns of harvesting operations in 
1998, TFL 6 provided more than 935 direct full -time equivalent jobs in 1998.  The economic and 
social benefits from TFL 6, combined with the Western Forest Products environmental stewardship 
program have resulted in a model of coastal forest management. 
For more information on Western Forest Products Limited or TFL 6, visit the Company’s web site at 
www.westernforest.com. 
The view from Simpson 250 in the Holberg Operation looking west into 
Quatsino Sound entrance shows a mix of forest types.  Approximately 50 per 
cent of the TFL forest cover remains in old growth condition, and healthy 
second growth covers the rest.  Thirty per cent is not scheduled for harvest 
for environmental, social or economic reasons.

1.2 Operations Highlights 
Western F orest Products’ parent company, Doman Industries, had another difficult financial year in 
1998.  Year -end results were negatively affected by the depressed demand for hemlock lumber in 
Asian markets, continued low pulp prices, and high stumpage and logging  costs.  Losses for the 
year were $74.6 million compared to a $2.3 million loss in 1997.  Sales for the year were $779 
million compared to $737 million in 1997.  The outlook for 1999 was not expected to be significantly 
better in spite of improved lumber a nd pulp prices at year end.  The Company was successful in 
significantly reducing pulp and lumber inventories by year end.  Poor markets resulted in the 
Company harvesting only 68 per cent of total Annual Allowable Cut in its forest operations in 1998. 
Cost reduction and controlling an increasing bureaucratic and administrative burden was a major 
focus during the second half of the year.  Government provided some stumpage relief in an attempt 
to keep logging operations active, but early in the fourth quarte r all logging operations were shut 
down.  Joint Industry -Government committees and action teams were put in place to design relief 
packages that would allow the forest sector to return to profitability in 1999.  Appendix XIX 
summarizes the operating statis tics for the Doman group of companies.

2.4 Planning 
2.4.1 Land and Resource Management Plans 
During the year little progress was made by government to complete the Vancouver Island Land 
Use Plan process.  Planning experts were drawn together by government to study and assess 
practices in Special Management Zones.  Proposed development in the  two TFL 6 Special 
Management Zones requires special studies and added assessments before harvesting can take 
place.  The Company regularly reviews its practices in these areas. 
Forestry costs associated with higher level planning, including land and resou rce management 
planning and landscape unit planning, were $60,850. 
2.4.2 Forest Development Planning 
Forest Development Plans (FDP) were completed in all Operations according to Ministry of Forests’ 
Policies and Procedures.  The year saw the introduction of a re vised Ministry FDP template that set 
new information and mapping standards.  In order to improve the timeliness of FDP production and 
spread the administrative burden over more of the year, it was decided to split the two North 
Vancouver Island Regional Pl ans into four Forest Operation -based Plans.  These were Holberg 
Regular, Holberg Special Management Zone, Port McNeill, and Jeune Landing.  The preparation 
and production of FDP maps was also moved from Western Forest Products’ corporate office to a 
Port M cNeill mapping contractor.  This required conversion of the Company’s PAMAP GIS files to 
Microstation.   
A one -year extension to the approved FDP was requested and received in order to provide time to 
introduce these new plans.  Four new FDPs were submitte d during the year but only the Jeune 
Landing Plan was approved in the same year.  
Forest Development Plan preparation and compilation costs totalled $143,966.

3.1.3 Felling, Bucking, and Utilization Specifications 
3.1.3.1 Specifications 
Cutting permits outline the required and optional utilization standards.  In general: 
i. Maximum stump height of 30 cm on shortest side;  
ii. All old growth coniferous trees containing X grade logs or better will be utilized to a top 
diameter of 15 cm inside bark (10 cm for second growth) ; 
iii. All conifer trees or parts of these trees exceeding 3 m in length which contain X grade logs or 
better will be removed; 
iv. Logs or parts of logs less than 3 m in length and broken at ends are classed as breakage;  
v. Logs will not be bucked or trimmed in a manner that reduces grade. 
These obligations apply to all living and dead trees that meet the standards. Quality control is 
maintained by Western Forest Products’ staff and monitored through felled and bucked inspections 
and residue surveys.
